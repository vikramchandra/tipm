# Research: Amazon Says My Original Book is "Stolen" - How to Fight a False Copyright Claim

## Overview of the Problem

Amazon's Kindle Direct Publishing (KDP) platform has a serious vulnerability: **anyone can file a copyright infringement claim against any book, and Amazon will immediately take it down**—often suspending or terminating the author's entire account within minutes, without verification.

> "Copyright infringement presents a big problem for authors, because Amazon makes it incredibly easy for anyone to claim copyright infringement against anyone else. Anyone can easily claim that your book is violating copyright, and Amazon KDP will immediately take it down, and likely suspend your account as well."
> — [Kindlepreneur](https://kindlepreneur.com/how-to-fight-copyright-infringement/)

> "The problem is that anyone can claim copyright infringement, and Amazon tends to shoot first and ask questions later."
> — [KBoards Forum](https://www.kboards.com/threads/how-do-i-prove-to-kindle-i-own-the-rights-to-my-own-book.336952/)

### Why This Happens

- Amazon uses automated systems (bots) to screen content, which frequently generate false positives
- Human review is minimal—most actions are triggered automatically
- The system prioritizes fraud prevention over author protection
- Amazon maintains intentional vagueness in its policies to combat scammers, but this harms legitimate authors

### How KDP's Internal System Actually Works (First-Hand Account)

From author Petey Lao's detailed account of his experience:

**Bot-First Enforcement:**
> "KDP heavily uses bots and algorithms to screen published work in their database. Many authors are blindsided by this as almost everyone flies through KDP's pre-publication review, only to be hunted down by bots on the back end. The bots are notorious for over-accusation, hence get things wrong quite frequently. But that's not KDP's problem. The burden of proof is on the authors."

**Globally Distributed Reps with Scripted Responses:**
> "All the KDP reps are located throughout the world. Every KDP email I received came from a different agent, even though it's the same email thread on the same subject. It was explained to me that this is by Amazon's design so they can minimize human subjectivity. To further de-personify their reps, KDP assigns them very limited templates of verbiage. The reps cannot deviate from the scripts and cannot really elaborate more on any details. Almost all communications are vague and often downright misleading."

**The Correction Trap:**
> "A lot of authors I know tried to correct their mistakes based on ambiguous feedback from KDP, only to be slapped in the wrist for more allegations because of their attempts to rectify original offenses."

**The Hostage Situation:**
> "Because KDP still held distribution rights to the book, even though they would not sell it on Amazon, I would not be able to take my manuscript to another distributor. My book was held hostage in Amazon KDP forever. And because my account was shut down by KDP in bad standing, no other distributor would accept my future work. I was officially a blacklisted author!"

- **Later September:** Changed cover again to new Shutterstock image (open book with magic). This triggered the bots.
- **Within 2 days:** Hit simultaneously by TWO different KDP departments:
  - Content review team: accused of using "materials freely available on the internet"
  - Account team: claimed use of "materials to which I did not have the rights"

- **October 24:** Attorney sent demand letters to 3 potential KDP legal department addresses

**What Finally Worked:**

- Two weeks after the letter, Amazon's law firm responded
- Account reinstated shortly after

## Common False-Claim Patterns

### 1. Competitor Abuse
Competitors file false claims to knock successful books off the market. This is weaponized business sabotage.

> "In the end, Amazon restored their KDP account. The claim was completely false, and it turned out that it was just a competitor wanting to knock their book off the market."

### 2. AI-Generated Duplicates
Scammers use AI to:
- Create fake books under real authors' names
- Publish summaries/workbooks of new releases within hours of publication
- Generate "biographies" that plagiarize memoirs

**High-Profile Victims:**
- Jane Friedman
- Kara Swisher (*Burn Book*)
- Stephanie Land (*Maid*)
- Marie Arana (*LatinoLand*)
- Roxana Robinson (*Leaving*)

**Source:** [Authors Guild](https://authorsguild.org/news/ai-driving-new-surge-of-sham-books-on-amazon/)

### 3. Algorithm/Bot Errors
Amazon's automated systems frequently misidentify legitimate content as infringing:
- Books with similar titles (even by the same author)
- Books with rear table of contents
- Books with similar cover designs
- Previously published books re-uploaded after rights reversion

> "KDP heavily uses bots and algorithms to screen published work in their database. Many authors are blindsided as almost everyone flies through KDP's pre-publication review, only to be hunted down by bots on the back end. The bots are notorious for over-accusation and get things wrong quite frequently."

### 4. Public Domain Confusion
Authors publishing public domain works (or works based on them) receive false claims from others who wrongly believe they own the content.

### 5. Title/Cover Similarity Claims
Amazon flags books with:
- Similar titles to existing books
- Similar cover designs
- Similar descriptions
- Similar author names

> "You could literally take almost any self-published book and argue the cover is similar."

### 6. Pen Name Confusion
Authors using pen names are particularly vulnerable. When Amazon's system sees a different name on copyright documents vs. the author name on file, it can trigger false claims.

From Kat Ross's experience: She had a book published with Amazon's own imprint (Amazon Skyscape) that proved Katherine Stapp = Kat Ross, held ISBNs, and had contracts for foreign/audio rights - yet Amazon still pulled her book and demanded copyright registration.

**Key advice from KBoards:** "If you responded to the email in a timely manner (they normally give you five business days), and gave them your real name the account is under and clearly stated you're using a pen name, then you can get someone to realize a mistake happened."

### 7. Cover Image Changes Triggering Bots
Changing your book cover - even to a legitimately purchased stock image - can trigger Amazon's bots.

Petey Lao's experience: After changing his cover twice (both times using properly licensed Shutterstock images), Amazon's bots flagged him. The second cover change resulted in simultaneous accusations from TWO different KDP departments with contradictory claims.

## Step-by-Step Dispute Process That Works

### Immediate Actions (First 24-48 Hours)

1. **Don't panic.** Read the notification carefully and document everything.

2. **Identify the specific issue.** What exactly is Amazon claiming?

3. **Do NOT submit multiple appeals.** This can hurt your case.

4. **Gather evidence immediately:**
   - Copyright registration certificate (if you have one)
   - Screenshots of your ISBN account showing ownership
   - Invoices/bank statements for editing, cover design, formatting
   - Email correspondence with editors, designers, beta readers
   - Original manuscript files with creation dates
   - Any contracts or reversion letters from previous publishers

### The Appeal Process

**Step 1: Craft Your Appeal**
- Be professional, polite, and concise
- Acknowledge the issue directly
- Provide clear evidence of your ownership
- Explain why the claim is false
- Describe what you'll do to prevent future issues

**Step 2: Submit via the Link in Your Notice**
Use only the official channel provided by Amazon.

**Step 3: Request Phone Contact**
Go to Author Central > Contact Us and request a phone call. Speaking to a live human can help.

**Step 4: Wait (But Follow Up)**
- Amazon quotes 5 business days for review
- Reality: Can take weeks to months
- Send polite follow-ups weekly if no response

**Step 5: Escalate if Needed**
- Email andy@amazon.com (CEO office) — "This has actually worked for some authors"
- Contact KDP on Twitter/X
- Reach out to the Authors Guild for assistance
- Consider legal representation

### Documentation That Works

**Most Effective:**
- Approved copyright registration from copyright.gov (not just the application)
- Screenshots of your ISBN account (Bowker/Nielsen) showing your name as owner
- Distributor affidavits (IngramSpark, Draft2Digital, PublishDrive)

**Sometimes Effective:**
- Editing/design invoices with supplier confirmation
- Website links and author bio pages
- Bank statements showing payments for services

**NOT Sufficient:**
- Personal statements of ownership
- The copyright page in your book
- Unsigned contracts
- Unregistered copyright applications

### Timeline Expectations

| Situation | Typical Timeline |
|-----------|------------------|
| Simple appeal with good documentation | 1-2 weeks |
| Complex case, no legal help | 1-3 months |
| With attorney involvement | 2-6 months |
| Authors Guild intervention | 24 hours - 2 weeks |

## What DOESN'T Work (And Wastes Time)

1. **Sending multiple appeals.** This flags you as problematic and can delay resolution.

2. **Being confrontational or aggressive.** Amazon support responds better to professional, factual communication.

3. **Expecting quick automated resolution.** The system that blocked you is automated; getting unblocked requires human review.

4. **Relying on your copyright page.** Amazon explicitly states: "The copyright page included in the book content is not sufficient to prove that you hold the necessary publishing rights."

5. **Going through standard infringement forms for name/likeness issues.** These are designed for copyright, not right-of-publicity cases.

6. **Waiting for Amazon to contact you.** Be proactive.

7. **Ignoring the 5-day deadline.** Respond promptly even if you don't have all documentation ready—send what you have and explain more is coming.

8. **Signing a "sworn statement of guilt" when you're innocent.** KDP sometimes demands authors sign statements admitting wrongdoing. Petey Lao signed one under pressure and it didn't help - he was still terminated later. It may actually hurt your case if you later need legal recourse.

9. **Trying to republish a blocked book with minor changes.** From KBoards: "Don't ever try to get around a blocked/banned book by changing something slightly and republishing it. They lost their accounts."

10. **Emailing Jeff Bezos.** Despite what some suggest, Petey Lao reported: "I emailed Jeff Bezos (jeff@amazon.com) almost daily to plead my case. He never returned my email." It's a waste of time.

11. **Contacting KDP executives on LinkedIn.** "I found two KDP executives on LinkedIn. Both were authors. I sent them passionate letters explaining my situation from one author to another. Both letters were returned to me unopened."

## Preventive Measures Authors Should Take

### Before Publishing

1. **Register your copyright with the US Copyright Office ($45-$65)**
   - Provides legal standing for court proceedings
   - Creates prima facie evidence if registered within 5 years of publication
   - Required to use the Copyright Claims Board
   - Some believe scammers check for registrations before targeting books

2. **Purchase your own ISBN ($125 for 1, $295 for 10 from Bowker)**
   - Establishes you as the official publisher of record
   - Creates indisputable documentation of ownership
   - "In the absence of registered copyright documentation, the best option is your ISBN record."

3. **Keep meticulous records**
   - Save all contracts, emails, invoices
   - Keep original manuscript files with creation dates
   - Document your writing process (early drafts, notes)
   - Screenshot your dashboards regularly

4. **Consider an LLC**
   - Legally separates personal assets from business liability
   - Protects personal property from copyright damage claims

### After Publishing

5. **Monitor for piracy and fraud**
   - Set up Google Alerts for your book title and author name
   - Use plagiarism detection tools periodically
   - Check Amazon listings for suspicious similar titles
   - Search for your name on Amazon to catch fake books

6. **Enable DRM (optional)**
   - Reduces casual sharing
   - Not foolproof against determined pirates
   - Can create inconvenience for legitimate readers

7. **Join professional organizations**
   - Authors Guild (can intervene with Amazon)
   - Alliance of Independent Authors (ALLi)
   - These provide resources and sometimes direct assistance

8. **Don't publish exclusively on Amazon**
   - Spread across multiple platforms (IngramSpark, Draft2Digital, Kobo, Apple Books)
   - If Amazon terminates your account, you still have income elsewhere

9. **Consider the IngramSpark Workaround**
   - If your KDP account is terminated or you want to avoid KDP's bot system entirely, publish through IngramSpark
   - IngramSpark distributes to Amazon, meaning your book appears on Amazon without being subject to KDP's internal review bots
   - As Petey Lao noted after his ordeal: "Wake will again appear on the Amazon website. The difference is, this time no Amazon bots or algorithms can touch it."

## Criticism of Amazon's System

### Industry Expert Criticism

**David Gaughran** (Indie Author Advocate, Kate Wilhelm Solstice Award recipient):
> "Amazon can invent flying delivery robots, but can't handle a 1990s-level internet marketer scam."

> "Amazon is undermining their own carefully built website infrastructure by allowing the gamers and scammers a place to flourish. They're eroding the confidence of their customers every time a reader clicks in and is faced with a wall of low-quality scam books."

**Mary Rasenberger** (Authors Guild CEO):
> "Misappropriating authors' names to sell scam books through Kindle and Goodreads is not new, but has gotten worse with the advent of AI-generated content."

**Justin Hughes** (IP Law Professor, Loyola Law School):
> "What we are seeing is, for authors, the prose equivalent of deep fakes."

### Systemic Problems

1. **No proper appeals mechanism.** Authors can't escalate beyond low-level customer service.

2. **Guilty until proven innocent.** Books are removed instantly; proving ownership takes weeks or months.

3. **Automated enforcement, human restoration.** Getting blocked is instant; getting unblocked requires human review that's understaffed.

4. **Vague violation criteria.** Amazon won't specify exactly what triggered the action, making defense difficult.

5. **No financial accountability.** Authors lose royalties during suspension with no compensation.

6. **Scammers move faster than enforcement.** "By the time Amazon finds out about them, they've already made some money."

### Amazon's Official Position

Amazon spokesperson Lindsay Hamilton stated that the company "investigates all concerns raised by authors" and that they "welcome author feedback and work directly with authors to address any issues they raise."

Amazon has implemented some measures:
- Limiting self-publishers to 3 books per day
- Requiring AI-generated content disclosure
- Suing external scammers (October 2023 lawsuit)

Critics say these measures are inadequate against sophisticated bad actors.

## Legal Escalation Options

### Option 1: Copyright Claims Board (CCB)
**Best for:** Disputes under $30,000

**Requirements:**
- Must have a registered copyright
- Works on small-claims court model

**Process:**
- File online at ccb.gov
- $40 filing fee + $60 if case becomes active
- No lawyer required
- Decisions made by 3-officer panel via videoconference
- Most work done in writing

**Limitation:** Respondent can opt out and force federal court instead.

**Source:** [US Copyright Office](https://www.copyright.gov/dmca/)

### Option 2: DMCA Counter-Notice
**Best for:** If Amazon removed your book due to a DMCA takedown

**Required elements:**
- Your physical or electronic signature
- Identification of removed material and its location
- Statement under penalty of perjury that removal was a mistake
- Your name, address, phone number
- Consent to federal court jurisdiction

**Timeline:**
- Amazon forwards counter-notice to original claimant
- Claimant has 10-14 days to file lawsuit
- If no lawsuit filed, Amazon must restore your content

**Warning:** Filing a counter-notice means you're willing to be sued. Only do this if you're certain you're in the right.

### Option 3: Attorney Representation

**When to consider:**
- Appeals have failed
- Significant income at stake
- False claim is causing ongoing harm
- You want to pursue damages against the false claimant

**Costs:**
- Initial demand letter: $300-$500
- Full representation: $2,500-$20,000+

**Finding an attorney:**
- AMZ Sellers Attorney (specializes in KDP issues)
- Creative Law Center (Kathryn Goldman)
- Local intellectual property attorneys
- LegalShield (subscription service providing attorney access—one author resolved their case for just $5.75 in postage using this service)

**Pro tip:** "Many authors who send a letter from a lawyer have their account reinstated quickly after the letter has been received by Amazon."

### Option 4: Authors Guild Intervention
**Best for:** Authors Guild members

**What they provide:**
- Direct communication channel with Amazon representatives
- Legal guidance
- Sometimes direct intervention

**Contact:** staff@authorsguild.org

### Option 5: Federal Court
**Best for:** Large damages, pattern of abuse, willful infringement

**Reality check:**
- Extremely expensive ($10,000-$100,000+)
- Takes years
- Requires registered copyright to sue
- Most cases settle before trial

## Key Takeaways for Article

1. **The system is broken.** Amazon's automated enforcement hurts legitimate authors while scammers adapt quickly.

2. **Preparation is protection.** Register your copyright, own your ISBN, keep records.

3. **Documentation wins appeals.** The authors who succeed have paperwork ready.

4. **Public pressure works.** When official channels fail, social media and industry organizations can move mountains.

5. **Legal threats get attention.** A lawyer's letter often resolves what months of appeals couldn't.

6. **Don't rely solely on Amazon.** Diversify your distribution to protect your income.

7. **Join author organizations.** The Authors Guild and ALLi can provide crucial support.

8. **This is getting worse, not better.** AI is accelerating the problem; regulatory solutions are lagging.
